Drugs Block Youth from Electoral Processes

Alarming levels of drug and alcohol abuse have been blamed for young people’s lack of interest in governance issues and electoral processes.

This comes at the backdrop of voter apathy in the recent by-elections, in a country where most of the eligible voters are young people.

According to advocate group, Youth Against Alcohol Dependency and Drug Abuse (YADD), alcohol and drug abuse are the major deterrence for youth participation in national processes.

“The fact that a large number of young people are hooked on drugs and alcohol and spend most of their time high means they end up missing opportunities to not only contribute in national dialogue but also participate in electoral processes,” said YADD founder Tungamirai Zimonte.

Zimonte who is also the country’s national coordinator for Southern Africa Alcohol Policy Alliance (SAAPA) said easy access of alcohol and hard drugs has seen more and more young people suffering from addiction.

“Illicit brews including fake whisky, vodka and other strong beverages that contain very high levels of alcohol have flooded the market at a very cheap price,” he said.

“One can get very drunk on a shared US 50 cents bottle of beverage. These brews are sold openly in many residential areas.

“Also readily available are dangerous drugs such as cough syrup and guka (crystal meth) which are highly addictive.”

The increasing level of drug and alcohol abuse which recently caught President Mnangagwa’s attention and that of various parliamentarians are largely attributed to high levels of youth unemployment and economic hardships.

“Most young people are turning to drugs as a result of frustration,” said clinical psychologist Tashinga Matsika.

“We live in a country where young graduates cannot find jobs while the economic conditions are very tough and the cost of living rising every day.

“Unfortunately instead of channelling the frustration towards adding their voice in shaping the future of the country, many young people have fallen to drug and alcohol abuse.”

Various anti-drug advocacy initiatives are being spearheaded in different constituencies.

Efforts have also been made towards arrest of drug peddlers.

Ordinary citizens are however of the view that the government is not doing enough to stop the drug epidemic.

“There are a lot of well-known drug dealers in our area, yet no arrests are made even when we do report them,” said Mbare resident Eliza Phiri, whose son is addicted to crystal meth.

“Sometimes we even see police officers at the premises of drug loads, which makes us suspect that either the drug dealers are politically connected or they pay bribes to police officers.”

Police are also accused of arresting the lowly peddlers leaving the drug masterminds to operate freely.
